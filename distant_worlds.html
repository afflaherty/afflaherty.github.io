Distant Worlds - A Small Analysis

Prompted by a comment regarding the Distant World series of music albums from Final Fantasy, I wanted to see if Final Fantasy VI in particular actually needed any more representation. To do this, I went through each album and labeled each track with the series it originated from along with the track length in seconds, cataloging the data in MS Excel. There are a number of tracks that are not attributed to a particular title or are a compilation of music from multiple titles. These tracks have been categorized separately under a general header. The aggregate data is compiled in Table 1.

It should be noted that while the total time of Final Fantasy VI does qualify as an outlier, it will not be excluded due to the limited size of the data set and the importance of the data point. That does make the outcome of the investigation more interesting considering how much it skews the data in its favor. To visualize the outcome, I converted each value into a z-score in order to see the raw deviation from the respecitve means for each title. This can be seen in Figure 1 and Figure 2.

Based on these deviations, it is clear the Final Fantasy VI has far more representation in the Distant Worlds albums than any other title, including the general category. This is ultimately unsurprising considering FFVI has at least one track on every album and encompasses all three tracks surpassing ten minutes. Obviously, the titles that would need greater representation are the ones that currently don't have any, those being FFI and FFII. Otherwise, outside of a general celebration of the series, the results point to four titles that Distant Worlds are FFVI and the Playstation era of games, FFVII, FFVIII, and FFIX.

Two other titles are worth noting, first being FFXIV, which has positive deviation in both figures but trails behind the frontrunners in terms of track representation, though it is on par with FFIX for the amount of time devoted to it. The second title is FFV, which has positive track representation but negative time representation. This is the only title that seems to be favored in terms of iconic tracks but are too short to compete with the grander pieces in later titles. I imagine something similar would occur if the earlier titles started getting more track representation due to the inherent restrictions on the development of those titles.
